Seminar Paper Presentation

AI, Quality Management, and the Future of Accounting Firms

In what ways is artificial intelligence reshaping quality management procedures and shaping future advancements in accounting firms?

Read the Paper Explore Chapter 2

Abstract

The ongoing integration of artificial intelligence is also fundamentally transforming the field of auditing by significantly expanding the possibilities for analyzing large amounts of data, pattern recognition, and process automation. This seminar paper addresses the research question of how AI-related application areas and challenges can be systematically integrated into the logic of quality management systems, particularly in the context of the International Standard on Quality Management 1. Methodologically, the paper follows a qualitative-conceptual approach. The study is based on a systematic literature review modeled after vom Brocke et al. (2015) and Webster and Watson (2002). The topics identified from the selected academic sources were synthesized to answer the research question and subsequently categorized using a conceptual matrix. The results identify six key application areas, including risk assessment, fraud and anomaly detection, and automated reporting. These potentials are encountered by critical challenges such as poor data quality, algorithmic bias, and the lack of transparency in complex models (the “black box” problem). The discussion makes it clear that AI must be understood not only as a technological tool but also as a central element of governance and risk management within a QMS. In summary, this study demonstrates that AI can sustainably improve audit quality and efficiency, provided that audit firms adapt their governance structures and make targeted investments in the professional development of their employees to combine technological innovations with professional judgment and critical thinking.

Keywords: Artificial Intelligence, Auditing, ISQM 1, Quality Management System, Audit Quality, Risk Management.

Chapter 1

Introduction

Artificial intelligence (AI) has become an integral part of our daily lives. AI products from major providers such as OpenAI and Anthropic. Global AI investments are reaching new heights, and the major pioneers in the field, such as Anthropic and OpenAI, whose products are already permeating the economy, government agencies, and people’s private lives, (are reportedly preparing for their initial public offerings as early as 2026 (Saini and Biswas, 2026). Similarly, it is increasingly becoming a significant factor in the field of accounting and auditing. While digital technologies and data analysis methods have been used for decades to support individual audit procedures (Messier and Hansen, 1987, pp. 97–101), AI significantly expands the possibilities for analysis, automation, and decision support. Modern AI systems can process large amounts of data, recognize patterns, identify anomalies, and support reporting and documentation processes (Kokina et al., 2025, pp. 7–8) . In doing so, they not only change individual operational audit activities, but also raise broader questions regarding the organization, management, and quality assurance of audit processes.

This trend is particularly significant in light of increasing demands for audit quality. Regulatory requirements and the desire for more cost-effective services are shaping client demand, and audit firms are under pressure to conduct audits efficiently, consistently, and in a risk-based manner, while at the same time ensuring the transparency, reliability, and independence of their audit opinions (DeFond and Zhang, 2014, p. 313). Quality management systems (QMS) provide a central organizational framework for this purpose. The International Standard on Quality Management 1 (ISQM 1) is an example of this, requiring audit firms to place greater emphasis on a risk-oriented, proactive, and continuous approach to quality management. Audit firms must identify quality risks, develop appropriate responses, allocate resources appropriately, define responsibilities, and monitor the effectiveness of their QMS (IAASB, 2020a, paras. 6–9). As a result, quality is not viewed merely as the outcome of individual audit engagements, but as an integral part of the entire organization that must be systematically managed.

The growing use of AI in auditing affects these requirements on several levels. The existing literature already discusses AI in auditing from various perspectives. Numerous studies examine potential areas of application, efficiency gains, and technological developments. The challenges that the use of AI can entail are also addressed. However, there has been less systematic research into the impact of AI on QMS in auditing firms. It often remains unclear how AI-related application areas and challenges can be concretely integrated into the logic of a QMS and what implications this has for the future management of audit quality.

This study addresses this research gap. The aim is to identify key areas of application and challenges associated with AI in auditing and then discuss them in terms of their significance for QMS in auditing firms. The focus is on ISQM 1, as it represents a key global standard for QMS in audit firms. In addition, the theoretical section considers Quality Management Standard 1 (IDW QMS 1), issued by the Institute of Public Auditors in Germany (IDW, German: Institut der Wirtschaftsprüfer in Deutschland e.V. ). It also includes a practical example of a QMS based on publicly available information from a large audit firm, in order to contextualize both the regulatory and practical framework.

This objective gives rise to the following research questions:

1. How can AI-related application areas and challenges in auditing be mapped to quality management systems in accounting firms?

2. What implications arise for future audit quality management?

To address this research question, this paper adopts a literature-based approach. First, the theoretical foundations of AI and selected subfields such as machine learning, deep learning, and generative AI are explained. AI is then contextualized within the field of auditing, before the fundamentals of QMS in auditing firms are presented. The focus is particularly on ISQM 1, supplemented by IDW QMS 1, as well as selected publicly available information on quality management at a major audit firm.

Building on this, a systematic literature review is conducted, methodologically based on Brocke et al. (2015) and, additionally, on Webster and Watson (2002). The aim of this research is to identify and evaluate relevant academic contributions on the use of AI in auditing and to structure them in a concept matrix. The concept matrix serves to systematically capture key application areas and challenges of AI use in auditing. The results of this analysis form the basis for the subsequent discussion, in which the identified categories are related to the components and requirements of ISQM 1. The thesis is structured as follows. Following this introduction, the second chapter presents the theoretical foundations. This chapter begins with an introduction to AI and relevant technological subfields. Subsequently, the use of AI in auditing is contextualized. Furthermore, QMS in auditing firms are explained, with ISQM 1 serving as the central reference point and supplemented by the national version in Germany as well as a practical example. The third chapter describes the methodological approach to the systematic literature review and the structuring of the literature using a conceptual matrix. The fourth chapter presents the results of the literature review. In doing so, key AI application areas and challenges in auditing are identified. The fifth chapter discusses these results in light of ISQM 1 and analyses the potential significance of AI for future quality management processes in audit firms. Finally, the sixth chapter reflects on the limitations of the study and provides an outlook on further research needs, while the seventh chapter summarizes the key findings and delivers a final conclusion.
