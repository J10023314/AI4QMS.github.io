The aim of this discussion is to place the AI-related application areas and challenges in auditing identified in the literature within the context of ISQM 1. This will examine the extent to which AI supports, alters, or introduces new quality risks to existing QMS in accounting firms.

5.1 AI as a Governance and Accountability Challenge

Following the perspective of the content of paragraph 28 of ISQM 1 (2020a), management should take responsibility for managing the challenges associated with AI, including ethical challenges, making appropriate decisions, and communicating these both internally and externally. As such, it bears a central responsibility for how AI is used within the audit firm and may ultimately also be morally and legally accountable for its use. To this end following ISQM 1, clear responsibilities and accountability structures for the use of AI must be established. Furthermore, it must be ensured that the necessary resources for its use are provided. This applies in particular to training employees in the use of AI and educating them about its limitations. When considering the use of AI in the context of the EU AI Act, the question arises to whether such systems should be classified as high-risk systems and thus subject to specific testing or certification requirements. While the audit itself does not fall directly under the high-risk areas defined in the framework (European Commission, 2026), clients that use AI systems in an audit context may fall into such categories, such as companies in the defence industry. Thus, raising the responsibility of governance for the leadership of accounting firms. For national frameworks in the EU that are based on ISQM 1, like the IDW QMS 1, this becomes especially important and leading to the question of necessary revision of those frameworks. Against this backdrop, the principles of Ethical AI and Explainable AI should be taken into account to ensure compliance with ethical and legal standards and to create transparency regarding the functioning and limitations of AI systems. This is particularly important for maintaining trust in the audit profession even as audit processes transition toward AI-supported methods.

However, AI also offers leadership opportunities to support more efficient management of quality management and to strengthen the quality QMS. This refers primarily to the opportunities discussed in more detail in the following subsections.

5.2 AI as a Resource and Competence Challenge

The implementation of AI in accounting firms already leads to changes in the structure of audits in terms of auditors’ levels of experience and the skill sets they should possess. There is a growing demand for young professionals with a technical and scientific background to meet the needs of a modern, data-driven professional environment (Leocádio et al., 2025, p. 11; Vitali and Giuliani, 2024, pp. 6–7). These skills are essential because staff must also be adequately trained in AI tools, with an understanding of how AI works to ensure the explainability and transparency of AI decisions, as well as addressing the issue of accountability. Investing in the competencies of auditors could also have a positive effect on the competence of the firm itself. And as DeFond and Zhang (2014, p. 313) note, the competence level of an audit is an important factor influencing the delivery of higher audit quality.

Furthermore, experienced auditors could pose a barrier to the acceptance of AI due to an aversion to new technologies. Junior staff, on the other hand, may be more prone to overreliance and, due to limited expertise, may have difficulty critically evaluating and questioning AI results, which can result in a deskilling in auditor competencies (Seethamraju and Hecimovic, 2023, p. 791). This means that different challenges may arise depending on the level of experience. Therefore, audit firms need to establish measures that foster a realistic acceptance of AI, while preventing both excessive scepticism and uncritical reliance. Employees must be empowered and motivated to use AI within its limitations and to trust it appropriately. However, it is particularly important to underline that AI must not replace the professional scepticism of experienced auditors. Even though AI can help analyse data more quickly and identify anomalies, it will remain the auditor’s responsibility to critically evaluate the results and interpret them within the specific audit context. Responsibility for the audit therefore remains with the auditor and cannot be transferred to an AI system as of now. AI can support the audit process, but it does not replace the professional judgment, responsibility, and accountability of the auditor and the auditing firm.

In the future, if AI develops to a certain level of reliability and acceptance, this assumption could change, especially if AI-generated results become more reliable and precise than those of human auditors. In such cases, accountability could lie with the legal entity that decided to use the AI, or, in certain cases, with the developer, for example in cases of negligence during the training process. For the foreseeable future, however, this will not call into question the importance of professional human judgment.

5.3 AI as part of the Firm’s Risk Assessment Process

With regard to the risk assessment process, it is evident from the results of the literature research that the use of AI can assist auditors in identifying and evaluating risks to the quality objectives and in finding potential responses to them. In particular, AI-supported predictive analytics and the incorporation of more analytical data through full-scope sampling enhance the potential for efficiency gains. This particularly strengthens risk assessment as well as fraud and anomaly detection. The use of more data enables more informed decisions regarding the selection and continuation of engagements and more efficient resource planning, allowing for a more precise assessment of the firm’s own capabilities and the existing risks associated with engagement execution. Thus, AI also changes the nature of engagement preparation and execution.

However, the results show that the use of AI in itself can be a potential risk. Thus, it requires that it be integrated into quality objectives and that risk scenarios, including applicable responses, be developed for it to address the challenges and risks identified in the literature review. This is intended to meet the new requirements for traceability, documentation, and critical evaluation of results.

5.4 AI in Engagement Performance and Information Processing

In the execution phase itself, AI can provide further support, for example by checking documents for completeness and accuracy before filing and retrieving them more quickly, or by conducting inventories using computer vision, as described in the results in the Sections 4.1.5 and 4.1.6. Also, it allows for the automation of standardized reports to inform Stakeholders. This relieves auditors of some of the burden of tedious routine tasks and enables them to make more informed decisions and focus their efforts more on strategic activities (Qatawneh, 2025, p. 1402). However, as mentioned in Section 5.2, the effective implementation of AI depends on an understanding of the technology itself and the user’s acceptance of it.

Although such applications illustrate the potential of AI-supported information extraction and processing, their reliable use in audit practice may still be limited and will be in the focus of development for the future. Evidence from other industries also shows that AI-based inventory systems can fail when they are not sufficiently accurate or when they disrupt existing work processes, like in the case of a system deployed by Starbucks (Rogelberg, 2026). Such examples further underline that AI-supported audit procedures require careful testing, human oversight, and integration into existing workflows before they can be relied upon in practice.

5.5 AI in Monitoring and Remediation

With regard to the monitoring and remediation process, AI can help support the requirements of para. 35 through 47 of ISQM 1 (2020a) and make their implementation more efficient. This becomes particularly evident in the category of “Continuous Auditing and Monitoring” as AI is what makes a continuous monitoring process in audit practice possible on a larger scale. While traditional monitoring processes are often more point-in-time or occur in retrospective, AI can analyse engagements on an ongoing basis and thereby identify risks and potential deficiencies earlier. Furthermore, it can assess these risks and deficiencies and, on this basis, develop or at least propose appropriate responses like shown in the results. Thus, AI cannot replace the Monitoring and Remediation Process, but as a supporting tool, it can help to make relevant anomalies visible more systematically and in a timelier manner. The communication of results can also be supported by AI. Through automated reports, the relevant information described in para. 46 of ISQM 1 (2020a) can be prepared in a standardized manner and communicated to the necessary stakeholders. This allows for a more structured sharing of information within the QMS and makes it easier to ensure that identified risks, deficiencies, and potential responses are not only recognized but also forwarded to the appropriate departments.

However, at the same time AI is not merely a potential support for the monitoring and remediation process. It must itself become the subject of this monitoring. The challenges described in Section 4.2 require continuous human oversight of AI usage. This is necessary to identify and, where possible, prevent shortcomings such as algorithmic bias or data breaches. Thus, the use of AI does not lie outside the QMS but must itself be integrated into its monitoring and improvement processes. Only in this way can it be ensured that the use of AI takes place within the framework of the ISQM 1 guidelines while also taking into account legal requirements, such as those set forth in the EU AI Act.

5.6 Implications for Future Quality Management Systems

In the future, AI-driven accounting firms could be able to achieve higher levels of audit quality. Mainly through the inclusion of all available and permitted data for analysis, automation of repetitive tasks and the enabling of continuous monitoring and reporting. But Manita et al. (2020, p. 6) came to the conclusion that existing quality management standards in accounting firms leave unanswered questions regarding the adoption and use of AI and could thus hinder potential improvements in audit quality. The ISAAB, on the other hand, following a conference with 240 stakeholders in the global audit industry, concluded that its standards (e.g. ISQM 1) provide a strong foundation for dealing with disruptive technologies like AI, but noted that stakeholders have expressed a desire for additional guidance (IAASB, 2026).

A central aspect of this discussion is the ethical dimension of AI use in auditing and its implications for QMS under ISQM 1. The use of AI in auditing gives rise not only to technical and organizational challenges, but also to ethical issues that are relevant to audit quality and public confidence in the audit profession. The use of AI must therefore be structured in such a way that data protection, confidentiality, and the requirements for handling sensitive information are upheld. Furthermore, bias or incomprehensible results can compromise the objectivity of the audit. If AI results are not sufficiently explainable or are based on biased data, there is a risk that decisions will be made on a basis that is professionally or ethically problematic.

Major changes are to be expected in the characteristics of the auditor profession, subsequently changing human resource management of accounting firms. Changes can be expected in the skill profile of future employees, how they are trained and how they are deployed. Technical skills and soft skills like critical thinking will be central characteristics of the future auditor. Furthermore, there is a risk that auditors will become over reliant on AI and are less inclined to maintain a professional scepticism about the results, which could lead to errors, a decline in audit quality and a deskilling of the auditor profession. Consequently, accounting firms have to implement these changes to foster an organizational culture, where auditors have the means, the confidence and the technology-acceptance to efficiently use AI in their daily tasks and deliver a higher quality of audit. In the future this could change if AI becomes more reliable and trusted, whereby less human oversight is required and expected.

Future QMS should therefore focus on higher investments of resources into the training of auditors in terms of competencies with AI, while also maintaining skill levels in the audit profession. Furthermore, leadership of accounting firms should invest in new and revised governance structures for the use of emerging technology like AI to upheld public trust in audit and be in accordance with legal and ethical standards. A promising area of research for this purpose is the research area of Ethical AI, especially the area and methods of XAI.
