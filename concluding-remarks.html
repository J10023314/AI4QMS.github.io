6. Limitations and Outlook

The findings of this study show that AI offers various points of integration for quality management in accounting firms, while simultaneously giving rise to new requirements regarding governance, control, and accountability. Nevertheless, the findings must be viewed in light of certain limitations. These relate in particular to the available literature, the conceptual nature of the study and the dynamic evolution of AI technologies.

The first limitation arises from the available literature. The literature review has shown that, to date, there is a gap of research examining the use of AI directly in connection with QMS in accounting firms. For this reason, the scope of the study was expanded to include the use of AI in auditing and accounting as a whole. The application areas and challenges identified from this were subsequently applied to the QMS according to ISQM 1. While this allows relevant implications for QMS to be derived, the results are based in part on a conceptual application rather than on studies that directly examine this relationship.

Another limitation is that the study provides a qualitative and conceptual classification. The focus is on the structured analysis of existing literature as well as the classification of the results within the context of ISQM 1. A quantitative assessment of the extent to which AI actually contributes to the effectiveness of QMS in accounting firms is not conducted within the scope of this study. This concerns, for example, the question of whether and to what extent AI improves audit quality, reduces quality risks, or enables efficiency gains within the QMS. In the near future, in particular, it can be expected that companies will evaluate their AI investments and assess their return on investment, including accounting firms. Especially on this point of time, an empirical or quantitative study offers a potential starting point for future research.

Furthermore, the scope of the study is limited by the rapid technological development of AI. These applications, particularly in the areas of generative AI, data-analytical audit procedures, and automated documentation processes, are constantly evolving. As a result, both the potential applications and the associated risks for accounting firms can change within a short period of time. The results of this study therefore reflect the current state of research and the trends that can be derived from it. However, they allow only limited fundamental assumptions or reliable predictions regarding the future significance of AI for QMS in auditing.

7. Summary and Conclusion

This paper examined in what ways AI is reshaping QMS and future advancements in accounting firms. Since the existing literature only rarely addresses the direct relationship between AI and QMS, the analysis was based on AI-related application areas and challenges in auditing. These findings were then discussed in relation to ISQM 1 in order to assess their implications for audit quality management.

The results show that AI is relevant in several areas of the audit process, including risk assessment and planning, fraud and anomaly detection, full population testing, audit procedures and reconciliation, reporting and documentation, as well as continuous auditing and monitoring. In these areas, AI can support accounting firms by processing large amounts of data, identifying patterns, automating routine tasks, and providing audit-relevant information more efficiently. Therefore, AI can contribute to audit quality when it is implemented appropriately and used as a support tool for professional judgment.

At the same time, the use of AI also creates challenges that are especially relevant for QMS. These challenges mainly concern data quality, reliability, transparency, explainability, confidentiality, ethical requirements and the role of human skills and judgment. AI-based results depend on the quality of the underlying data and may be difficult to understand or verify. For this reason, AI does not reduce the need for professional scepticism, but rather reshapes the skillset of auditors to someone who can critically assess, document and understand AI-generated outputs. However, if AI systems reach a higher level of accuracy and reliability in the future and gain broader public trust, the need for human oversight may decrease, as AI could increasingly perform tasks autonomously.

In relation to ISQM 1, AI should therefore not only be understood as a technological tool to facilitate advancements in audit quality and effectiveness, but also as a governance and quality risk issue. Its use can affect several components of QMS, especially leadership responsibilities, ethical requirements, resources, information and communication, engagement performance, and the monitoring and remediation process. Audit firms need to establish appropriate responsibilities, policies, documentation requirements, and control mechanisms to ensure that AI supports audit quality instead of creating unmanaged risks.

Overall, the analysis shows that AI can support quality management in accounting firms, but its benefits depend on how it is integrated into an existing QMS. The responsibility for audit quality remains with the accounting firm and the responsible auditor. AI systems can assist audit procedures, but they cannot replace professional judgment, professional scepticism, or ethical responsibility. Future audit quality management will therefore depend on the ability of audit firms to combine technological innovation with adequate governance, human expertise, and continuous monitoring.
